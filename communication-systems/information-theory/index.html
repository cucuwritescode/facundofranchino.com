Information Theory Introduction

by Facundo Franchino

Introduction

Information theory, developed by Claude Shannon in 1948, provides the mathematical foundation for understanding how information can be measured, stored, and transmitted efficiently.

The Concept of Information

Information can be quantified mathematically. The information content of an event with probability $p$ is defined as:

$$I(x) = -\log_2(p(x)) \text{ bits}$$

For example, if we flip a fair coin:

  • Probability of heads: $p = 0.5$
  • Information content: $I = -\log_2(0.5) = 1$ bit

Entropy

The entropy of a discrete random variable $X$ represents the average information content:

$$H(X) = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} p(x_i) \log_2 p(x_i)$$

where $p(x_i)$ is the probability of outcome $x_i$.

Properties of Entropy

  1. $H(X) \geq 0$ (entropy is always non-negative)
  2. $H(X)$ is maximum when all outcomes are equally likely
  3. $H(X) = 0$ when one outcome has probability 1

Applications in Communication Systems

Information theory provides fundamental limits for:

  • Source coding: How efficiently can we compress data?
  • Channel coding: How reliably can we transmit over noisy channels?
  • Channel capacity: What's the maximum rate of reliable communication?
