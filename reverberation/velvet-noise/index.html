Velvet Noise A Technical Overview

by Facundo Franchino

Helsinki, Finland

Introduction

Velvet noise is a sparse sequence of impulses whose spacing is randomised about a fixed mean interval. It provides an efficient means of generating smooth, natural-sounding textures from minimal data, forming the basis of several modern artificial reverberation and decorrelation algorithms.

Mathematical Structure

Formally, the sequence of impulse times is defined as:

$$t_n = nT + \delta_n$$

where:

  • $T$ is the mean period between impulses,
  • $\delta_n$ is a zero-mean random deviation, and
  • $n$ is the impulse index.

The resulting signal is:

$$v(t) = \sum_{n} a_n \, \delta(t - t_n)$$

where $a_n \in \{-1, +1\}$ are randomly assigned amplitudes.

At low densities, this produces isolated clicks. As the density $\lambda = 1/T$ increases, the ear perceives a continuous texture rather than discrete impulses. The effect resembles a smooth hiss, although its energy is carried by far fewer samples than white noise.

In discrete time, the signal is commonly implemented as:

$$v[n] = \sum_{k} a_k \, \delta[n - n_k]$$

with event indices $n_k = kN + \epsilon_k$, where $N = \mathrm{round}(f_s T)$, $f_s$ is the sampling rate, and $\epsilon_k$ are small integer jitters.

Efficiency and Reverberation

The efficiency of velvet noise arises from its sparsity. Because most of the samples are zero, it can synthesise large, diffuse sound fields with very little computation.

When the impulses are shaped by filters or multiplied by a decaying envelope $e^{-\alpha t}$, the output resembles the fading reflections of an acoustic space. From these minimal elements one can construct the perceptual illusion of continuous decay, allowing digital systems to recreate realistic reverberant behaviour.

Decorrelation Applications

Velvet noise also functions as a decorrelation signal. Two independent sequences, each defined by distinct random deviations $\delta_n$, are nearly uncorrelated yet perceptually similar. When applied to multiple audio channels, this property disrupts coherence without introducing tonal colouration, producing a natural stereo or surround diffusion.

Summary

Velvet noise provides a compact, perceptually smooth foundation for artificial reverberation and spatial diffusion. It demonstrates how precisely constrained randomness can produce stability and realism in audio processing.
