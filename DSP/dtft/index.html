On Transforms The Discrete Time Fourier Transform (DTFT)

by Facundo Franchino

In our previous entry, we established that signals are patterns carrying information, and that our brains (much like computers) are constantly filtering and analysing these inputs. But how do we actually see these patterns?

If we stare at a waveform in the time domain (amplitude over time), we see the shape of the wave, but the pitch, among various other characteristics, remain hidden. To reveal them with a computer, we need a tool to transform the signal into a time-frequency domain representation.

This leads us to a fundamental tool in modern Digital Signal Processing, the Discrete Time Fourier Transform (DTFT).

Sampling... Reality

To process a signal digitally, we must first compromise infinite time. Our continuous signal will be sliced into discrete moments, which we shall call samples.

Imagine a continuous signal, g(t). To digitise it, we sample it at regular intervals. Mathematically, this is equivalent to multiplying our smooth, continuous signal by a train of 'impulses' (Dirac deltas), mathematical spikes that exist only at specific moments T, 2T, 3T, and so on.

Conclusion

The DTFT is mathematically elegant, but it is computationally impossible. It relies on infinite sums that no machine can calculate.

To make this tool useful, we must discretise the frequency domain just as we discretised time. Next, we leave the blackboard for the terminal and turn this theory into a runnable algorithm: the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT).
