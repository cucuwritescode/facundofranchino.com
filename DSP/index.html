On The Elementary Principles of Digital Signal Processing Introduction

by Facundo Franchino

What is Signal Processing?

Signal processing is how we represent, analyse and modify signals. These are patterns that carry information.

Our Brains are Signal Processors, by Nature!

Scenario 1: The Cocktail Party Effect

Imagine you are at a noisy gathering. You want to hear your friend speaking, but there's chatter all around. Your brain somehow filters out noise, so you can understand your friend. Your brain is performing signal processing, taking a complicated jumble of sound and discriminating the parts that don't matter at will.

Scenario 2: Musical Transcription

When we learn harmony and ear training, we are effectively learning to retrieve intricate information from complex signals. One could say the brain is a far more powerful signal processor for certain tasks than modern computers. For instance, it was a rather trivial task for a composer like Mozart to transcribe complex polyphonic chorale arrangements and masses on one hearing, most famously Gregorio Allegri's Miserere, while this remains largely an unresolved problem in Automatic Music Transcription (AMT).

But, Where's Signal Processing in Everyday Life?

To process a signal is to handle it with intention. We may wish to uncover what it contains, to reduce what we deem irrelevant, or to reshape it for another purpose.

Clever signal processing is ubiquitous in today's world. When you Shazam a song, compress an image so it fits on your phone, use Radar to track a flight, add digital reverb to your guitar track from either a pedal or a plugin, or tune the radio, you are effectively utilising signal processing.

Signals can be complex, but hidden within them are patterns and relationships which one, as a signal processing engineer, may exploit. Signal processing provides the mathematical tools to reveal said structures, to isolate what matters, and to reconstruct information in a more useful form.
